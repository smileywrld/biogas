The problem

Waste left to rot is waste left to harm

Biodegradable materials thrown away in the open don't just disappear — they become a slow-burning hazard to health and environment.

🪰

Pests & Disease

Rotting food waste attracts flies and vermin, raising the risk of disease spreading through the community.

🌬️

Foul Odors

Open decomposition releases unpleasant smells that lower quality of life for everyone nearby.

🌲

Lost Resources

The same waste, left unused, means continued reliance on firewood — and continued pressure on our forests.

How biogas is produced

From scraps to fuel in five steps

Biogas is a clean, renewable fuel produced when biodegradable materials decompose without oxygen, inside a sealed container called a biodigester.

Collection of Waste

Biodegradable materials — food waste, vegetable peels, animal manure, and plant residues — are gathered.

Preparation

The waste is mixed with water to form a slurry.

Digestion

The slurry is poured into an airtight biodigester, where bacteria break it down without oxygen.

Gas Production

As decomposition occurs, gases are released — chiefly methane, which is combustible and usable as fuel.

Collection & Usage

The gas is collected and stored for cooking, lighting, or generating electricity.

Benefits to the community

One digester, four wins

01 — Energy

Clean, affordable fuel

Provides a clean and affordable source of energy for cooking, lighting, or generating electricity.

02 — Environment

Less pollution

Helps reduce environmental pollution by converting waste into useful fuel instead of letting it rot in the open.

03 — Forests

Less firewood demand

Reduces the amount of firewood used for cooking, helping to protect our forests.

04 — Sanitation & Soil

Cleaner surroundings, richer soil

Improves sanitation by reducing waste accumulation, and the leftover material from the digester can be used as organic fertilizer to improve crop production.
